Chapter 6 - Frequentist Statistics

  • Statistical inference that avoid treating parameters like random variables, and which thus avoid the use of priors and Bayes rule.
  • Instead of being based on the posterior distribution, they are based on the concept of a sampling distribution.
  • This is the distribution that an estimator has when applied to multiple data sets sampled from the true (but unknown) distribution.
  • It is this notion of variation across repeated trials that forms the basis for modeling uncertainty used by the frequentist approach.
  • By contrast, in the Bayesian approach, we only ever condition on the actually observed data; there is no notion of repeated trials. This allows the Bayesian to compute the probability of one-off events.

Sampling distribution of an estimator

  • In frequentist statistics, a parameter estimate θ^\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}θ^ is computed by applying an estimator δ\deltaδ to some data D\mathcal{D}D,so θ^=δ(D)\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}=\delta(\mathcal{D})θ^=δ(D).

  • The parameter is viewed as fixed and the data as random, which is the exact opposite of the Bayesian approach.

  • The uncertainty in the parameter estimate can be measured by computing the sampling distribution of the estimator.

  • To compute the sampling distribution of the estimator, there are two ways:

    • The bootstrap is a simple Monte Carlo technique to approximate the sampling distribution. Create N datasets by sampling subsets of the original dataset, compute the estimate from each sample; then we have an empirical distribution of the estimator. The set of estimates obtained is used (in the Frequentist realm) as an alternative to sampling different θ\boldsymbol{\theta}θ from the posterior distribution (Bayesian).
    • Large sample theory for the (maximum likelihood) estimate: It can be shown that, under certain conditions, as the sample size (number of subsets) tends to infinity, the sampling distribution of the MLE becomes Gaussian. The center of the Gaussian will be the MLE θ\boldsymbol{\theta}θ. Standard error which is an estimate of our uncertainty about our estimate, can be obtained (analytically) as the inverting the fisher information matrix. Frequentist decision theory

  • In frequentist or classical decision theory, there is a loss function and a likelihood, but there is no prior and hence no posterior.
  • We first choose any estimator or decision procedure δ:X→A\delta : \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathcal{A}δ:X→A
  • Having chosen an estimator, we define its expected loss or risk as follows:
    R(θ∗,δ)≜Ep(D~∣θ∗)[L(θ∗,δ(D~))]=∫L(θ∗,δ(D~))p(D~∣θ∗)dD~R\left(\theta^{*}, \delta\right) \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{p\left(\tilde{\mathcal{D}} | \theta^{*}\right)}\left[L\left(\theta^{*}, \delta(\tilde{\mathcal{D}})\right)\right]=\int L\left(\theta^{*}, \delta(\tilde{\mathcal{D}})\right) p\left(\tilde{\mathcal{D}} | \theta^{*}\right) d \tilde{\mathcal{D}}R(θ∗,δ)≜Ep(D~∣θ∗)​[L(θ∗,δ(D~))]=∫L(θ∗,δ(D~))p(D~∣θ∗)dD~
    where D~\tilde{\mathcal{D}}D~ is data sampled from “nature’s distribution”, which is represented by parameter θ∗\theta^{*}θ∗ (the true parameter, unknown!). In other words, expectation is wrt the sampling distribution of the estimator. In comparison, in Bayesian, the posterior expected loss is:
    ρ(a∣D,π)≜Ep(θ∣D,π)[L(θ,a)]=∫ΘL(θ,a)p(θ∣D,π)dθ\rho(a | \mathcal{D}, \pi) \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{p(\theta | \mathcal{D}, \pi)}[L(\theta, a)]=\int_{\Theta} L(\theta, \mathbf{a}) p(\theta | \mathcal{D}, \pi) d \thetaρ(a∣D,π)≜Ep(θ∣D,π)​[L(θ,a)]=∫Θ​L(θ,a)p(θ∣D,π)dθ
  • We see that the Bayesian approach averages over θ\thetaθ (which is unknown) and conditions on D\mathcal{D}D (which is known), whereas the frequentist approach averages over D~\tilde{\mathcal{D}}D~ (thus ignoring the observed data), and conditions on θ∗\theta^{*}θ∗ (which is unknown).
  • Not only is the frequentist definition unnatural, it cannot even be computed, because θ∗\theta^{*}θ∗ is unknown. Consequently, we cannot compare different estimators δ\deltaδ in terms of their frequentist risk.

Desirable properties of estimators

For choosing on the best estimator.
Unfortunately, we will see that we cannot achieve all of these properties at the same time!

  1. Consistent estimators
  • An estimator is said to be consistent if it eventually recovers the true parameters that generated the data as the sample size goes to infinity, i.e., θ^(D)→θ∗ as ∣D∣→∞\hat{\theta}(\mathcal{D}) \rightarrow \theta^{*} \text { as }|\mathcal{D}| \rightarrow \inftyθ^(D)→θ∗ as ∣D∣→∞
  • It can be shown that the MLE is a consistent estimator. The intuitive reason is that maximizing likelihood is equivalent to minimizing KL(p(⋅∣θ∗)∥p(⋅∣θ^)), where p(⋅∣θ∗)\mathbb{K} \mathbb{L}\left(p\left(\cdot | \boldsymbol{\theta}^{*}\right) \| p(\cdot | \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}})\right), \text { where } p\left(\cdot | \boldsymbol{\theta}^{*}\right)KL(p(⋅∣θ∗)∥p(⋅∣θ^)), where p(⋅∣θ∗), where p(⋅∣θ∗)p\left(\cdot | \boldsymbol{\theta}^{*}\right)p(⋅∣θ∗) is the true distribution and p(⋅∣θ^)p\left(\cdot | \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\right)p(⋅∣θ^) is our estimate. We can achieve 0 KL divergence iff θ^=θ∗\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}=\boldsymbol{\theta}^{*}θ^=θ∗.
  1. Unbiased estimators
  • The bias of an estimator is defined as bias⁡(θ^(⋅))=Ep(D∣θ∗)[θ^(D)−θ∗]\operatorname{bias}(\hat{\theta}(\cdot))=\mathbb{E}_{p\left(\mathcal{D} | \theta^{*}\right)}\left[\hat{\theta}(\mathcal{D})-\theta^{*}\right]bias(θ^(⋅))=Ep(D∣θ∗)​[θ^(D)−θ∗] where θ∗\theta^{*}θ∗ is the true parameter.
  • If the bias is zero, the estimator is called unbiased. This means the sampling distribution is centered on the true parameter.
  • For example, the MLE μ^\hat{\mu}μ^​ for a Gaussian mean μ\muμ is unbiased. However, the MLE for a Gaussian variance, σ^2\hat{\sigma}^{2}σ^2, is not an unbiased estimator of σ2\sigma^{2}σ2.
  • For large enough NNN, the difference will be negligible. Although the MLE may sometimes be a biased estimator, one can show that asymptotically, it is always unbiased. (This is necessary for the MLE to be a consistent estimator.)
  1. Minimum variance estimators

  • Not only that we would like the estimator to be unbiased, we would also like it to have a low variance (when computed across subsets of the data).

  • Cramer-Rao lower bound, provides a lower bound on the variance of any unbiased estimator.
    var⁡[θ^]≥1I(θ∗)\operatorname{var}[\hat{\theta}] \geq \frac{1}{I\left(\theta^{*}\right)}var[θ^]≥I(θ∗)1​
    where III is the Fisher Information Matrix.

  • MLE achieves the Cramer Rao lower bound, and hence has the smallest asymptotic variance of any unbiased estimator. Thus MLE is said to be asymptotically optimal.

Bias-Variance Tradeoff:
Although using an unbiased estimator seems like a good idea, this is not always the case. To see why, suppose we use quadratic loss (i.e., the risk is given by MSE).
Let θ^=θ^(D)\hat{\theta}=\hat{\theta}(\mathcal{D})θ^=θ^(D) denote the estimate, and θ‾=E[θ^]\overline{\theta}=\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}]θ=E[θ^] denote the expected value of the estimate (as we vary D\mathcal{D}D).

We can decompose the MSE as :
E[(θ^−θ∗)2]=E[[(θ^−θ‾)+(θ‾−θ∗)]2]=E[(θ^−θ‾)2]+2(θ‾−θ∗)E[θ^−θ‾]+(θ‾−θ∗)2=E[(θ^−θ‾)2]+(θ‾−θ∗)2=var⁡[θ^]+bias⁡2(θ^)\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\hat{\theta}-\theta^{*}\right)^{2}\right] &amp;=\mathbb{E}\left[\left[(\hat{\theta}-\overline{\theta})+\left(\overline{\theta}-\theta^{*}\right)\right]^{2}\right] \\ &amp;=\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\overline{\theta})^{2}\right]+2\left(\overline{\theta}-\theta^{*}\right) \mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}-\overline{\theta}]+\left(\overline{\theta}-\theta^{*}\right)^{2} \\ &amp;=\mathbb{E}\left[(\hat{\theta}-\overline{\theta})^{2}\right]+\left(\overline{\theta}-\theta^{*}\right)^{2} \\ &amp;=\operatorname{var}[\hat{\theta}]+\operatorname{bias}^{2}(\hat{\theta}) \end{aligned}E[(θ^−θ∗)2]​=E[[(θ^−θ)+(θ−θ∗)]2]=E[(θ^−θ)2]+2(θ−θ∗)E[θ^−θ]+(θ−θ∗)2=E[(θ^−θ)2]+(θ−θ∗)2=var[θ^]+bias2(θ^)​

In other words, MSE=variance+bias2\mathrm{MSE}=\mathrm{variance}+\mathrm{bias}^{2}MSE=variance+bias2

  • What it means is that it might be wise to use a biased estimator, so long as it reduces our variance, assuming our goal is to minimize squared error.

Empirical risk minimization

  • Frequentist decision theory suffers from the fundamental problem that one cannot actually compute the risk function, since it relies on knowing the true data distribution.

  • However, there is one setting which avoids this problem, and that is where the task is to predict observable quantities (yyy's), as opposed to estimating parameters θ\thetaθ.

  • That is, instead of looking at loss functions of the form L(θ,δ(D))L(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \delta(\mathcal{D}))L(θ,δ(D)), where θ\thetaθ is the true but unknown parameter, and δ(D)\delta(\mathcal{D})δ(D) is our estimator, let us look at loss functions of the form L(y,δ(x))L(y, \delta(\mathbf{x}))L(y,δ(x)), where yyy is the true but unknown response, and δ(x)\delta(\mathbf{x})δ(x) is our prediction given the input x\mathbf{x}x. In this case, the frequentist risk becomes:
    R(p∗,δ)≜E(x,y)∼p∗[L(y,δ(x)]=∑x∑yL(y,δ(x))p∗(x,y).R\left(p_{*}, \delta\right) \triangleq \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{x}, y) \sim p_{*}} \left[L(y, \delta(\mathbf{x})\right]=\sum_{\mathbf{x}} \sum_{y} L(y, \delta(\mathbf{x})) p_{*}(\mathbf{x}, y).R(p∗​,δ)≜E(x,y)∼p∗​​[L(y,δ(x)]=x∑​y∑​L(y,δ(x))p∗​(x,y).

  • where p∗p_{*}p∗​ represents “nature’s distribution”. Of course, this distribution is unknown, but a simple approach is to use the empirical distribution, derived from some training data, to approximate p∗p_{*}p∗​, i.e.,
    p∗(x,y)≈pemp(x,y)p_{*}(\mathbf{x}, y) \approx p_{\mathrm{emp}}(\mathbf{x}, y) p∗​(x,y)≈pemp​(x,y)

  • We then define the empirical risk as follows:
    Remp(D,D)≜R(pemp,δ)=1N∑i=1NL(yi,δ(xi))R_{e m p}(\mathcal{D}, \mathcal{D}) \triangleq R\left(p_{\mathrm{emp}}, \delta\right)=\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} L\left(y_{i}, \delta\left(\mathbf{x}_{i}\right)\right)Remp​(D,D)≜R(pemp​,δ)=N1​i=1∑N​L(yi​,δ(xi​))

  • This is essentially the loss function averaged across all data points.

    • In the case of 0-1 loss, L(y,δ(x))=I(y≠δ(x))L(y, \delta(\mathbf{x}))=\mathbb{I}(y \neq \delta(\mathbf{x}))L(y,δ(x))=I(y​=δ(x)), this becomes the misclassification rate.
    • In the case of squared error loss, L(y,δ(x))=(y−δ(x))2L(y, \delta(\mathbf{x}))=(y-\delta(\mathbf{x}))^{2}L(y,δ(x))=(y−δ(x))2, this becomes the mean squared error.

  • We define the task of empirical risk minimization or ERM as finding a decision procedure δ\deltaδ (the best set of model parameters) that minimizes the empirical risk:
    δERM(D)=argmin⁡δRemp(D,δ)\delta_{E R M}(\mathcal{D})=\underset{\delta}{\operatorname{argmin}} R_{e m p}(\mathcal{D}, \delta)δERM​(D)=δargmin​Remp​(D,δ)

  • In the unsupervised case, we drop yyy from the loss function. Example, the reconstruction loss: L(x,δ(x))=∥x−δ(x)∥22L(\mathbf{x}, \delta(\mathbf{x}))=\|\mathbf{x}-\delta(\mathbf{x})\|_{2}^{2}L(x,δ(x))=∥x−δ(x)∥22​ such as in an autoencoder, δ(x)=decode⁡(encode⁡(x))\delta(\mathbf{x})=\operatorname{decode}(\operatorname{encode}(\mathbf{x}))δ(x)=decode(encode(x)). Of course, we can always trivially minimize this risk by setting δ(x)=x\delta(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}δ(x)=x, so it is critical that the encoder-decoder go via some kind of bottleneck.

Regularized risk minimization
  • Minimizing the empirical risk will typically result in overfitting. It is therefore often necessary to add a complexity penalty to the objective function (basically a regularization term)
    R′(D,δ)=Remp(D,δ)+λC(δ)R^{\prime}(\mathcal{D}, \delta)=R_{e m p}(\mathcal{D}, \delta)+\lambda C(\delta)R′(D,δ)=Remp​(D,δ)+λC(δ)
  • where C(δ)C(\delta)C(δ) measures the complexity of the prediction function δ(x)\delta(\mathbf{x})δ(x) and λ\lambdaλ controls the strength of the complexity penalty.
Structural risk minimization
  • In the above, how should we pick λ\lambdaλ? We cannot using the training set, since this will underestimate the true risk, a problem known as optimism of the training error (i.e., the model will not generalize).
  • Instead we pick λ\lambdaλ that minimizes an estimate of the risk R^(δ^λ)\hat{R}\left(\hat{\delta}_{\lambda}\right)R^(δ^λ​) (which can be computed either using cross-validation or theoretical upper bounds on the risk)
  • Estimating the risk using cross validation: (a) Find loss on validation set; (b) K-Fold cross validation; (c) Leave-one out cross validation.
  • Upper bounding the risk using statistical learning theory:
    • The principle problem with cross validation is that it is slow, since we have to fit the model multiple times. This motivates the desire to compute analytic approximations or bounds to the generalization error (studied in a field called statistical learning theory).
    • One can extend statistical learning theory by taking computational complexity of the learner into account. This field is called computational learning theory or COLT.
    • If we observe a low empirical risk, and the hypothesis space is suitably “small” (wrt model parameters or model complexity), then we can say that our estimated function is probably approximately correct or PAC. A hypothesis space is said to be efficiently PAC-learnable if there is a polynomial time algorithm that can identify a function that is PAC function.
Surrogate loss functions
  • Minimizing the loss in the ERM framework is not always easy. For example we might want to optimize AUC or F1 score.
  • Or more simply, we might just want to minimize the 0-1 loss, as is common in classification. Unfortunately, the 0-1 risk is a very non-smooth objective and hence is hard to optimize. One alternative is to use maximum likelihood estimation instead ( it can be shown that log-likelihood is a smooth convex upper bound on the 0-1 risk).
